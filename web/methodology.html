01. Purpose

About this tool

This is a read-only OSINT (open-source intelligence) aggregator focused on Russia-related shadow-fleet maritime activity. It overlays a live AIS feed with publicly published sanctions lists, ownership-graph data, news mentions, and Wikidata records; computes a transparent multi-factor risk score per vessel; flags proximity events that fit ship-to-ship transfer patterns; and infers cargo and voyage intent from declared destination + draught.

It is intended as a research and journalistic aid. It is not legal evidence, regulatory enforcement, or a substitute for due diligence by a regulated party. Every claim is sourced and explainable — risk factors are listed inline in the side panel; sanction list entries link back to the upstream source; ownership chains link to OpenSanctions; news links open the original article.

Allegations vs verified claims. When this tool says a vessel is "sanctioned", it means the vessel's IMO appears on at least one publicly published sanctions or detention list. Inclusion does not by itself prove wrongdoing; appeals and de-listings happen. When it says a vessel "may be conducting STS", it describes an observed proximity pattern, not a proven event. When it says a vessel was "previously named X", it cites the OpenSanctions record; we do not run our own registry investigations.

03. Scoring

Risk scoring

Every vessel gets a 0–100 composite risk score. The score is a sum of named factors, capped at 100. Each factor's contribution is shown inline in the vessel detail panel — there is no opaque ML model.

+ 5 per sanctions list (cap 30)
+ 20 if Russia / Ukraine sanctions linked
+ 10 if flag of convenience
+ 10 if currently AIS-dark (>60 min)
+ 15 if AIS gap inside STS / sanctioned-port zone (24h)
+ 5 if longest 24h gap >2h
+ 10 if ownership chain touches sanctioned-state owner (RU / IR / KP / BY / SY / VE)
Vessel-profile & behaviour signals (recon, v3):
+ 5–15 flag-hopping (≥2 prior flags on FtM record, scales to cap)
+ 5–10 ageing hull (≥15y / ≥20y)
+ 10 Port State Control detention in last 24 months (Paris / Tokyo MoU)
+ 15 suspected AIS position spoofing (implausible jump in own track)
+ 10 loitering near-stationary inside an STS / sanctioned zone
+ 10 named in a documented investigation (KSE / CREA / UANI / OCCRP)
→ capped at 100, bucketed into:
low < 30 elevated 30–59 high 60–79 critical 80+

Why these weights

The strongest signal in the open literature is being on multiple independent sanction lists — that's why list count compounds (5 × N, capped at 30). Russia/Ukraine linkage is weighted heavily because it's the most actionable category for current EU/UK enforcement. The chain-touches-sanctioned-state-owner factor (added in v2) catches vessels where the immediate registered owner is not on a list but their parent or beneficial owner is in a sanctioned-state jurisdiction — a textbook obfuscation pattern.

Recon signals (v3)

Six signals broaden coverage beyond the sanctions lists into the vessel's own profile and behaviour. Flag-hopping and ageing hull are derived from the OpenSanctions Follow-the-Money record (pastFlags, buildDate) — both are well-established dark-fleet markers. Port State Control detentions come from the public Paris MoU / Tokyo MoU lists (operator-loaded via load-psc) and are an independent measure of substandard tonnage. AIS spoofing and loitering are computed from our own position track — a spoofing jump is an implied speed above 40 kn between consecutive fixes; loitering is ≥60 min near-stationary (<1 kn) inside a suspect zone. Documented cases link a vessel to a published investigation (KSE / CREA / UANI / OCCRP, operator-loaded via load-cases); that table also serves as a ground-truth set for tuning these weights.

Flag-of-convenience list

Curated from ITF and KSE shadow-fleet research: Panama, Liberia, Marshall Islands, Cyprus, Malta, Bahamas, Belize, Bermuda, Cambodia, Comoros, Cook Islands, Djibouti, Gabon, Guinea, Honduras, Jamaica, Kiribati, Lebanon, Sri Lanka, Mongolia, Mauritius, Niue, Palau, São Tomé and Príncipe, Sierra Leone, Togo, Tonga, Vanuatu, Barbados.

What scores cannot tell you. A vessel with score 20 may still be conducting an STS event right now; a vessel with score 80 may have a clean recent voyage. The score is a triage prior — it tells an analyst where to look, not what to conclude. Always read the breakdown of factors before citing a score.

05. Detection

STS event detection

Ship-to-ship transfers are routine maritime operations, but they are also the textbook way Russian and Iranian crude is laundered between sanctioned tonnage and "clean" buyers. We approximate STS events from AIS proximity in two stages.

Stage 1 — candidate pairs

  1. Two tankers (AIS ship type 80–89) within 1.5 nm (configurable) of each other.
  2. Both at speed <2 kn (configurable).
  3. Position reports within 5 min of each other.
  4. At least one IMO is on any of our 50+ ingested sanction lists.

Stage 2 — event clustering

  1. Group consecutive observations of the same vessel pair into a single event.
  2. A gap of more than 30 min between observations starts a new event.
  3. Filter events shorter than 10 min (configurable) as noise.
  4. Tag the event's midpoint against the suspect-zone list.

Distance is computed via an equirectangular approximation of haversine — accurate to <0.1% at distances <50 nm. Both /api/sts-candidates (raw) and /api/sts-events (clustered) are exposed; the map and digest use clustered events.

False positives. Vessels at the same anchorage, in lock queues, or under tow can satisfy the proximity test without any STS happening. Vessels with the same MMSI broadcasting from different positions (spoofing) can also create phantom matches. Treat events without zone tagging as lower-confidence.
False negatives. Sophisticated operators turn off AIS during an STS. We capture the dark periods (see §06) but cannot directly observe what happens during them. SAR-satellite imagery is the next layer of verification — see the Sentinel-1 deep-link in §06.

06. AIS

AIS dark periods + Sentinel-1 SAR verification

A "dark period" is any interval >30 minutes between two consecutive AIS broadcasts from the same MMSI. For each vessel we compute gaps over the configured time range (1h–30d) and flag gaps whose start or end position falls inside a suspect zone.

Gaps can mean any of: intentional shutdown (shadow-fleet pattern), receiver coverage gap (open ocean), equipment failure, or AIS spoofing. We report gaps as observations and let the analyst weigh them in context.

Sentinel-1 SAR verification

For each AIS gap inside a suspect zone, we generate a deep-link to Copernicus Browser pre-filled with the gap's bounding box and a ±12-hour time window, set to the Sentinel-1 GRD dataset. SAR penetrates cloud cover and works at night, so it is the gold-standard way to verify whether a vessel was physically present during a dark period.

Why this matters. A vessel claiming AIS-equipment-failure for a 14-hour gap inside Lakonikos Bay is a different story if Sentinel-1 imagery from that window shows two tankers moored next to each other. We do not auto-fetch SAR imagery (Copernicus has its own access flow) — we just one-click the analyst into the right view.

07. Graph

Ownership graph

Ownership data comes from OpenSanctions FtM (Follow-the-Money), a public structured-data model used by most non-commercial sanctions trackers. Each entity (vessel, company, person, address) has a stable NK-... ID and is connected via typed relations (Ownership, Directorship, Membership, Family, Representation, Employment, UnknownLink).

For each vessel we run a breadth-first traversal of these relations, capped at 4 hops and 80 nodes per query. The traversal is bidirectional — both upstream owners and downstream sub-entities are surfaced.

Reverse search

The header search box queries entity captions across all 248k entities, with vessel-count prioritisation. Use it to drill into a known operator — e.g. "Sovcomflot" returns the parent plus the 84 vessels FtM links to it. Live AIS positions, where available, are highlighted in red.

What's not in the graph. Many vessels carry an owner property as free-text rather than a linked entity ID. These appear in the vessel detail sanction-source panel but cannot be expanded as graph nodes. To resolve them we would need OpenCorporates, Sayari, or proprietary registry access — out of scope.

08. Reconciliation

Sanctions list reconciliation

Across our two sanction-source layers (OFAC SDN direct + OpenSanctions Maritime aggregate) we have ~15,300 raw list entries which deduplicate to ~8,870 unique IMOs. The same vessel commonly appears on 3–7 lists.

  • Primary key: IMO when present (~99% of entries).
  • Secondary key: for entries without IMO, OpenSanctions provides a stable internal ID; we keep them but cannot join them to AIS.
  • Aggregated programmes: shown as a list per vessel — e.g. "OFAC + EU + UK + Canada + Ukraine + Tokyo MOU".
  • Russia/Ukraine flag: a vessel is flagged if any program label contains "Russia", "Ukraine", or "ua_war".

We do not currently track de-listing dates. A small fraction of our list entries may refer to vessels that have been removed from the upstream list since our last refresh. Reload OpenSanctions weekly to keep this delta small.

09. Inference

Cargo & voyage

Without commercial feeds (Kpler, Vortexa, Lloyd's List Intelligence) we cannot know exact cargo tonnage. We surface qualitative cargo intent from three signals:

Cargo type

Derived from the FtM type property — e.g. "Crude Oil Tanker" → 🛢️ Crude oil; "Oil Products Tanker" → ⛽ Refined petroleum; "Chemical Tanker" → ⚗️ Chemicals; "LNG Carrier" → ❄️ LNG. When FtM has no type we fall back to AIS ship_type code 80–89.

Load status (loaded / partial / ballast)

Inferred from the AIS-broadcast current draught compared to typical thresholds for the vessel category:

CategoryBallast (≤)Loaded (≥)
VLCC11 m19 m
Suezmax9 m16 m
Aframax / crude8 m13 m
Product tanker5.5 m10 m
Chemical tanker5 m9.5 m
LNG carrier9 m11 m
LPG carrier6 m10 m

Between thresholds = "partial" load. Reasoning is shown inline in the vessel detail.

Destination decoding (UN/LOCODE)

AIS destination is a free-text 5-character UN/LOCODE (e.g. RUULU = Russia / Ust-Luga). We curate ~110 codes covering Russian / Iranian / Venezuelan oil exports, STS hubs, major Chinese / Indian refineries, and global commercial ports. Compound destinations like RULED>EGSUZ are split into origin / destination. Phrases like "FOR ORDERS" or "OPL" are tagged with their tactical meaning (awaiting routing, off-port-limits anchored — both common shadow-fleet patterns).

10. Identity

Aliases & previous names

Frequent vessel-name changes are a textbook shadow-fleet operational pattern — they break tracker continuity and let operators escape stale-data sanctions screening. Two sources of historical names:

  • OpenSanctions FtM name array — every alias the upstream list publishers ever recorded, deduplicated by entity ID. The vessel's current AIS-broadcast name is filtered out, leaving prior names.
  • Wikidata SPARQL P458 — querying the vessel by IMO returns the Wikidata-canonical name, which often differs from both AIS and FtM. Useful for vessels with Wikipedia articles (Eagle S, Vezhen, etc.).

The vessel detail panel shows the union of both, with FtM's free-text notes block surfaced separately (it often contains explicit "Previous names include…" text from the underlying sanction-package PDF).

Worked example. The vessel currently broadcasting as CELT (IMO 9299692) has FtM aliases "CALLISTO" and "Callisto"; Wikidata records the same hull as "NS Concord". Three documented names on one IMO is not unusual for sanctioned tonnage.

12. Bulk

Researcher endpoints

For journalists, NGOs, and compliance researchers, the tool exposes structured endpoints with CSV export. Everything is reproducible from raw AIS + sanctions data — no closed-source enrichment.

Bulk feeds

GET /api/sanctioned-active?range=24h&lists=ru&format=csv

Currently visible sanctioned tankers with risk score, programmes, last position. Filters: range, lists (ofac, eu, uk, ua, ru, iran).

GET /api/sts-events?range=24h&format=csv

Discrete STS events (clustered candidates) with start/end, duration, min distance, observation count, zone tag.

GET /api/newly-active?since_days=7&sanctioned=true&format=csv

Vessels first observed in our AIS feed in last N days. Tracks new tonnage appearing in a region.

GET /api/newly-listed?since_days=180&ru=true&format=csv

Vessels added to OpenSanctions FtM in last N days. Tracks designation cadence.

GET /api/advanced-filter?min_risk=60&russia=true&in_zone=true&dark_min=120&format=csv

Combine criteria. Example query: "All Russia/Ukraine-linked vessels with risk ≥ 60 currently dark for >2h inside a suspect zone".

Aggregate analytics

GET /api/fleet-stats?range=24h

Flag distribution, build-year buckets, top owners by visible fleet, programme overlaps, zone-dwell minutes.

GET /api/owner-fleet-activity/:entityId?range=7d

Time-bucketed AIS activity for all vessels owned by a single entity (e.g. Sovcomflot, NITC).

Compliance bulk-screening

POST /api/batch-screen (up to 1,000 IMOs/request, JSON body)

Returns sanction status, sources, programmes, RU/UA flag, OS-canonical name (reveals aliases), AIS-broadcast name, and "currently visible" flag for each input IMO.

13. Caveats

Limitations & known issues

  • Position history depth is bounded by how long the ingestor has been running. Long-range views (7d / 30d / all) become meaningful only after the ingestor has been collecting for that length of time.
  • Geographic coverage is limited to the four configured AIS bounding boxes. Vessels operating outside these will not be tracked.
  • GDELT availability is best-effort. The endpoint may be unreachable from some networks. When it fails we display a graceful empty state and serve previous successful results from cache for 1h.
  • Wikidata coverage is sparse — most random tankers are not in Wikidata. Famous vessels (Eagle S, Vezhen, NS Champion) are.
  • Free-text owners in OpenSanctions metadata are not graphable.
  • Vessel name collisions exist — common names like FORTUNE or HALCYON match many vessels. Always verify by IMO.
  • Sanctions de-listings are not pulled in real time; reload OpenSanctions weekly.
  • STS detection is proximity-based, not SAR-confirmed. Treat as triage; use the Sentinel-1 deep-link to verify when needed.
  • Cargo inference (load status from draught) is heuristic. Real tonnage requires commercial feeds.
  • Risk score weights are heuristic and will be tuned against operator feedback. They are not derived from a backtested model.
  • Newly-listed uses OpenSanctions.createdAt, which is when OS first ingested the entity — a close proxy for "newly designated" but not exactly the same.
  • Port-call inference from AIS positions assumes vessels broadcast continuously while inside ports. Vessels that go AIS-dark on approach will not register a port call here.
